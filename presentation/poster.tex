\begin{block}{Motivation}
\justifying
Likert responses are ordinal, not cardinal. Estimating mean differences therefore imposes untestable measurement assumptions.

\vspace{0.45em}
\keybox{Target causal effects on the observed ordinal response distribution, not coefficients on an arbitrary latent scale.}

\vspace{0.55em}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.25em}
  \item Latent attitudes are observed only through ordered categories.
  \item Latent treatment effects are identified only up to location and scale.
  \item Category-specific and cumulative probability effects remain directly interpretable.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Ordinal Measurement and Estimands}

\vspace{-0.3em}
\justifying
The target object is the distribution of potential ordinal outcomes:
\[
\pi_j(d)=P\{Y_i(d)=j\}=\mathbb{E}_X\left[P(Y_i=j\mid D_i=d,X_i)\right].
\]
\vspace{-0.6em}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.25em}
  \item \textbf{Category-specific effects:} $\Delta_j=\pi_j(1)-\pi_j(0)$.
  \item \textbf{Cumulative effects:} $\Delta_{\geq j}=P\{Y_i(1)\geq j\}-P\{Y_i(0)\geq j\}$.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Estimation Problem}
\justifying
\begin{itemize}
  \item Ordered logit and ordered probit $\implies$ \textbf{fixed latent error distribution} and a \textbf{parametric index}. 
  \item When attitudes are polarized, clustered, skewed, or nonlinear in covariates, these assumptions can be violated.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.45em}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Approach & Strength & Limitation \\
\midrule
OLS & Simple & Assumes cardinal scores \\
Dichotomization & Easy & Discards ordering info \\
Ordered models & Respects ordering & Fixed error distribution \\
Flexible flows & Likelihood-based & Higher computational cost \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{block}{Proposed Framework}
\justifying
The estimator keeps the threshold-crossing structure but replaces restrictive parametric assumptions with a conditional normalizing flow.

\vspace{0.35em}
\begin{center}

\vspace{0.35em}
For each response category,
\[
P(Y_i=j\mid X_i,D_i)=F_\theta(\alpha_j\mid X_i,D_i)-F_\theta(\alpha_{j-1}\mid X_i,D_i).
\]
We estimate $\Theta = \{\tau, h, \phi, \alpha\}$ directly by maximizing the log-likelihood:
\[
\ell(\Theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=0}^{J} \mathbb{I}(Y_i=j) \log \left[ F_\theta(\alpha_j \mid X_i,D_i) - F_\theta(\alpha_{j-1} \mid X_i,D_i) \right]
\]
This provides a genuine likelihood rather than a surrogate loss, retaining exact inference.
\end{block}

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.25em}
  \item Ordered response models perform well when assumptions are approximately correct.

  \item Structured flow estimators improve robustness while retaining statistical efficiency.
\end{itemize}

\begin{block}{Empirical Replications}
\justifying
Two published survey experiments illustrate when flexible estimation confirms conventional findings and when it changes substantive interpretation.

\vspace{0.4em}
  \textbf{Human rights and support for military intervention(Tomz and Weeks, 2020)}\par
Estimated treatment effects are broadly stable across empirical, ordered response, and flow-based estimators, suggesting conventional models are adequate in this setting.

\vspace{0.25em}
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \maybeincludegraphics{width=0.98\textwidth}{../figures/fig_tomz_empirical.pdf}
  \caption{Category-specific effects in the Tomz replication.}
\end{figure}

\vspace{0.55em}
  \textbf{Authoritarian propaganda and public opinion (Mattingly et al. 2025)}\par
Estimated effects differ across modeling assumptions, especially in middle and upper response categories. Flexible distributional modeling changes which categories absorb the treatment-induced probability shift.

\vspace{0.25em}
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \maybeincludegraphics{width=0.98\textwidth}{../figures/fig_mattingly_empirical.pdf}
  \caption{Category-specific effects in the Mattingly replication.}
\end{figure}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Take Away}
\justifying
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.25em}
  \item \textbf{Report Probability-Based Quantities:} Avoid interpreting raw latent coefficients. Focus on category-specific ($\Delta_j$) and cumulative ($\Delta_{\geq j}$) probability effects.
  \item \textbf{Assess Distributional Sensitivity:} Use flexible flow estimators as a robustness check to ensure findings are not driven by arbitrary normal or logistic assumptions.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
